# PHANTOM GRID — SONIC BRIEF
## Positive Definition

This document defines what a Phantom Grid release sounds like. It is the counterpart to `not-phantom-grid.md`. Both documents are equally binding.

---

## TEMPO

**115–140 BPM** — the working range of the label. Not a genre boundary. A pressure range.

Below 115: permitted only when the reduced tempo carries the same machine gravity — not as ambient filler, not as decompression. Slow material must be as uncompromising as fast material. Ambient and minimal tracks are not exempt from discipline.

Around 140 and above: Techno territory. Valid. The label releases Techno, Minimal, and Ambient alongside Electro. BPM does not determine quality — intent does.

Detroit Electro was the spark. The label's range is wider: **Electro, Techno, Minimal, Ambient** — united by structural precision and machine logic, not by tempo or genre code.

---

## DRUM ARCHITECTURE

The drums are the argument. Everything else is the context.

- **Kick:** punchy, dry, minimal decay. Not booming. Not clicky. The weight of a machine, not a room.
- **Snare / clap:** sharp, slightly synthetic. Rim shots permitted. No acoustic snare emulation.
- **Hi-hat:** programmed, not performed. Patterns are mechanical — repetition is intentional, not lazy.
- **Percussion:** sparse and deliberate. Every additional element earns its place.

Reference machines: 808, 909, TR-707, Linndrum. The sound of these machines is not nostalgia — it is the correct vocabulary for the form.

---

## BASS

Low-end is structural, not decorative.

- Sub-bass presence: present but controlled. The bass must translate on small speakers as well as a club system.
- Acid bass lines (303 or equivalent): permitted, not required. When used: purposeful, not gratuitous. The acid line is a statement, not a hook.
- Bass movement: mostly repetitive with deliberate variation. The bass holds the transmission in place.

---

## SYNTHESIS

- **Pads and atmosphere:** cold, filtered, mechanical. Warmth is permitted in small doses — it creates contrast against the machine cold. Never lush, never cinematic.
- **Leads:** sparse. A Phantom Grid track does not need a lead. When one appears, it carries specific information.
- **Arpeggios:** acceptable when they function as rhythmic texture, not as melody.
- **Aquatic quality:** filtering, phasing, slow modulation — the Drexciya influence. Depth through movement, not reverb wash.

---

## SPACE AND MIX

- **Dry over wet:** this music exists in a machine environment, not a room. Reverb is used deliberately, not habitually.
- **Dynamic range:** compressed but not destroyed. The track must breathe even when it is relentless.
- **Stereo field:** wide on atmospheric elements, tight on rhythm. The kick is center. Always.
- **Silence:** intentional. A Phantom Grid track knows when to pull back. The space between elements is as important as the elements.

---

## ARRANGEMENT

- **Intro:** functional — it sets the groove and establishes the transmission. Not longer than necessary.
- **Development:** slow, deliberate. This music does not drop. It shifts. Elements enter and exit with purpose.
- **Peak:** earned, not manufactured. The most intense moment of a track arrives because the arrangement built to it, not because a filter opened.
- **Outro:** the transmission does not end — it fades into the frequency range where it came from.

Typical EP track length: **6–9 minutes.** Long enough to establish something. Short enough to mean it.

---

## THE CORE QUESTION

After three listens:

> *Does this music know what it is?*

Not: does it sound like Detroit Electro. Not: does it fit a genre.
The question is whether the transmission is coherent — whether the artist understood their own form deeply enough to say something that hasn't been said exactly this way before.

A Phantom Grid release can be Electro, Techno, Minimal, or Ambient. It cannot be undecided. It cannot be assembled from correct parts without an internal logic. Structure is not enough. The machine must have a reason to run.

If yes: the rest of this document is secondary.
If no: no amount of correct BPM or right machines will fix it.
